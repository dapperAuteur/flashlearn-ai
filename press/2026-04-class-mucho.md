### What the MUCHO course covers

The course is structured around real 360° photography captured during BAM's field-content trip to MUCHO Museo del Chocolate in Mexico City. Students rotate the view, follow guided learning prompts, and move scene-by-scene through the museum exhibits.

Anchor lessons:

- **The Olmec origin.** How cacao moved from a sacred crop in early Mesoamerican civilizations to a global commodity. Why "the food of the gods" was the original framing. What changed when the Spanish encountered it in the 16th century.
- **Chocolate as currency.** Cacao beans served as money in Aztec markets. The course walks through this period using the museum's display of period artifacts.
- **Industrial chocolate.** From craft Mesoamerican preparation to mass production. The Dutch press, milk chocolate, the cocoa-cocaine trade-route convergence in the 20th century.
- **Modern chocolate ethics.** Single-origin sourcing, Fair Trade, slavery in West African cocoa supply chains, the rise of bean-to-bar craft chocolate.
- **Tasting the difference.** A guided exploration of the museum's tasting room, including instructions for practitioners who want to do their own structured tasting at home.

### Why this format works

MUCHO is shot in 360°. Students don't watch a slideshow. They stand inside the museum's entrance hall, then the Olmec exhibit, then the tasting room. Each scene has a defined vantage point. Click anywhere in the scene to look around. The guided learning sequence walks through the exhibit at the student's pace.

For students who would otherwise need to fly to Mexico City to experience the museum in person, MUCHO is the next best thing. For students preparing to visit MUCHO in person (it's a real, public museum at Calle Milán 45, Colonia Juárez, Mexico City), the course is a deep-context primer that makes the in-person visit richer.

### Companion to the BVC chocolate class

Chocolate is one of the named vices in the Better Vice Club curriculum. The BVC chocolate class addresses chocolate from a behavioral / vice-engagement angle: when does chocolate earn its place in your week and when doesn't it? The MUCHO course addresses the same vice from a cultural / historical / sensory angle: where did chocolate come from and what does the bean actually do?

Together, BVC's chocolate class plus MUCHO gives a learner the framework + the experience for honest engagement with chocolate as a vice. Take them in either order. Most students take BVC first to build the framework, then MUCHO to deepen the context. Some take MUCHO first because the museum is a vacation hook and they back into the framework afterward.
